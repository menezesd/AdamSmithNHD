Previous Systems

Feudalism

Feudalism was the economic system used between A.D. 400 and A.D. 1400. Under feudalism, land was owned by nobles who provided protection as well as use of land for serfs. Serfs worked for a living and gave almost all the proceeds of the land to the nobles. After the Black Death between 1346 and 1351, feudalism was no longer self-sustaining.

Mercantilism

Previously, trade with Asia was dominated by the Italians. Because the Black Death first appeared in Italy, Europeans wanted a new land route to Asia. However, because the Ottoman Empire restricted trade by land, it was necessary to find a sea route:

The commercial revolution may be said arbitrarily to have begun in 1488 when the Bartholomew Diaz rounded Cape Horn and thus pioneered a non-Mediterranean route to trade that soon involved direct European contacts in the whole arc from Japan to the Persian Gulf. (Rostow 107)

After Bartholomew Diaz's voyage, exploration continued. Mercantilism formed the basis of colonization in the Americas. Under mercantilism, nations sought keep imports less than exports and to accumulate pecuniary wealth in the form of precious metals. The British imposed the Navigation Acts of 1651 on the American colonies in an attempt to give Britain a monopoly on imports and exports to the American colonies. The government strictly regulated production, and set limits on wages and the number of apprentices one could take:

Engraving of Adam Smith from Wikimedia Commons.

In 1666, a French law stated that "If a cloth weaver intends to process a piece according to his own invention, he must not set in on the loom, but hes should obtain permission from the judges of the town to employ the number and length of threads he desires, after the question has been considered by 4 of the oldest merchants and 4 of the oldest weavers of the guild." (Heilbroner 30)

In contrast to Smith's idea of the "invisible hand," followers of the mercantilist system held a negative view toward competition:

Instead of risk and change, the model was safety first. Advertising was forbidden, and the idea that one master guildsman might produce a better produce than his colleagues was regarded as treasonable. (Heilbroner 28)

Mercantilism was the dominant economic system between 1400 and 1800. Smith's Wealth of Nations and the beginning of the Industrial revolution led to mercantilism's downfall. In the Wealth of Nations, Smith argued against the mercantilist idea of artificially maintaining a trade surplus:

It is a maxim of every prudent master of a family, never to attempt to make at home what it will cost him more to make than to buy. The tailor does not attempt to make his own shoes, but buys them of the shoemaker. The shoemaker does not attempt to make his own clothes but employs a tailor.…

What is prudence in the conduct of every private family can scarce be folly in that of a great kingdom. If a foreign country can supply us with a commodity cheaper than we ourselves can make it, better buy it of them with some part of the produce of our own industry employed in a way in which we have some advantage. (Smith 194)

Smith used grapes to show the ridiculousness of the restriction of imports to encourage domestic industry (Photograph from Wikimedia Commons).

Smith argued that the notion of a "protective tariff" or other restriction on importation to encourage domestic industry is ridiculous and detrimental to the interests of the populace:

By means of glasses, hotbeds, and hot walls, very good grapes can be raised in Scotland, and very good wine too can be made of them at about thirty times the expense for which at least equally good can be brought from foreign countries. Would it be a reasonable law to prohibit the importation of all foreign wines merely to encourage the making of claret and burgundy in Scotland? (Smith 195)

The Physiocrats

The Physiocrats were a group of economists who, like Adam Smith, believed in individualism and laissez-faire. However, they believed that the wealth of nations was derived only from agriculture and regarded the work of artisans, manufactures, and merchants as "sterile." Smith felt that reduction in the number of artisans and manufactures would diminish the market for agricultural produce and thereby discourage agriculture:

Every system which endeavors, either by extraordinary encouragements to draw towards a particular species of industry a greater share of the capital of the society than what would naturally go to it, or, by extraordinary restraints, force from a particular species of industry some share of the capital which would otherwise be employed in in, is in reality subversive of the great purpose which it means to promote. It retards, instead of accelerating, the progress of society and greatness; and diminishes, instead of increasing, the real value of the annual produce of its land and labour. (Smith 300)
